Dealing with negative trial results

Welcome to the world of clinical trials. I think it’s great that you want to find new treatments that will improve human health. And let’s be honest, if you are a clinician, the career boost is nice as well. But I have some bad news for you. Are you ready?

Your intervention doesn’t work. I’m sorry. It doesn’t. Not even a little. No, not in the older patients. Not in the younger ones either. It…does…not…work.

Hold on a minute. You haven’t even run the trial yet. So how can I possibly know it doesn’t work? Experience my friend – and as any experienced trialist can tell you, the intervention never works. Ever. I don’t care about your pilot data. I don’t care about your theory or proposed mechanism. If I could find a way to short clinical trials like stock, I’d be filthy rich. So I’ll say it one last time: The intervention never works.

…

Ok, with that in mind, let’s go forward in time, to the end of the trial. You are standing next to me (the trial’s statistician) at the front of a large meeting room in an almost-really-nice-hotel. I have a clicker in my hand, there is a graph projected onto the large screen behind us, and I just told everyone that the intervention didn’t work.

Who is everyone? The entire study team of course, all sat quietly at a giant U-shaped table with little name placards in front of them. All the site investigators are there. There is someone from the DMSB too. And of course all the other people that just spent years of their lives working on the trial. Someone from the funding body is there as well - it seems they are interested in what you did with their money. And don’t forget the patients’ representative – they gave more than anyone.

OMG, look at their faces. They look so confused. Disappointed. A few even look sad. Oh no, are those tears? But we were just having so much fun, drinking free coffee and catching up since our last meeting. Now they are just staring at us. You have to say something. You have to say something now.

Freeze time

What are you going to say?

To survive this moment, you need to be able to look everyone in the eye and remind them that the trial was designed to rigorously answer an important question. If this is true, then you have likely just learned something useful, regardless of whether the intervention worked or not. Further, if the study was well designed, other people will be more likely to trust your result and act on it. In other words, there is no such thing as a “negative” trial result – the answer given by a well-designed trial is always useful, whether the intervention worked or not. So you simply remind everyone of this. People will still be disappointed of course - we’d be fools to test treatments if we didn’t think they worked (even though they never do). But at least we can all live with ourselves knowing that we gave it our absolute best. At least we all know that we added knowledge to the world.

And what if the trial wasn’t well designed? Then the errors in the design might be used to reasonably explain away any result, “positive” or “negative”. Maybe the intervention didn’t appear to work because the trial was too small. Or maybe it only appeared to work because you used a surrogate outcome. And regardless of the explanations you come up with, nobody else will believe the result anyway, so what’s the point? So the best case scenario is that you’ve wasted lots of time and money. The worst case scenario is that you have needlessly put patients at risk. Trust me, there’s not enough perfume in France to cover that kind of stink.

So here is my advice. At the start of any trial, imagine yourself in the situation above. The intervention didn’t work. How are you going to live with yourself? Now act accordingly.
